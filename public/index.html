What VSEPR theory says

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory starts from one idea: electron pairs around a central atom — whether they're in bonds or sitting as lone pairs — repel each other, so they arrange themselves in 3D to get as far apart as possible. The shape that results is what we call the molecular geometry.

Step 1 — Count electron domains (steric number)

An "electron domain" is anything occupying space around the central atom: a single bond, a double bond, a triple bond (each still counts as one domain — multiple bonds don't add extra repelling groups, they just act as one fat domain), or a lone pair.

Steric number = (number of atoms bonded to the central atom) + (number of lone pairs on the central atom)

Step 2 — Electron-domain geometry vs. molecular geometry

The electron-domain geometry is the arrangement of all domains, including lone pairs. The molecular geometry only describes where the atoms are — lone pairs are invisible in the final shape, but they still push the visible atoms around, which is why water (steric number 4, 2 lone pairs) is bent rather than tetrahedral-looking.

Step 4 — Lone pairs reshape (but don't relabel) the geometry

For a given steric number, swapping a bonded atom for a lone pair keeps the same electron-domain geometry and hybridization, but changes the molecular geometry name and usually compresses the angle slightly, since lone pairs are "fatter" (held closer to the nucleus, less shielded) and repel more strongly than bonding pairs:

Step 5 — How lone pairs are counted from a Lewis structure

This tool estimates lone pairs from each atom's valence electrons and how many of those electrons are tied up in bonds:

Lone pairs = (valence electrons − total bond order) / 2

where "total bond order" sums 1 for each single bond, 2 for each double bond, and 3 for each triple bond the atom participates in. The result, combined with the number of attached atoms, gives the steric number used in the table above.

Caveats

VSEPR predicts idealized angles. Real molecules often deviate by a few degrees because lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs, multiple bonds repel more than single bonds, and electronegativity differences pull electron density unevenly. VSEPR also doesn't handle transition-metal coordination complexes, extensively delocalized π-systems, or radicals well — treat the output here as a solid first approximation, not a substitute for computational chemistry or experimental data.

Contribute a compound

Add a compound's name, formula, and SMILES string here. It gets saved to the shared library on this server, so anyone who opens this app (and is connected to the same running instance) can type the same formula and immediately get your structure — including as a labeled option when a formula matches more than one known compound (isomers).

Don't know SMILES? It's a compact way to write structure as text: atoms are element symbols (C, O, N, Cl...), bonds default to single, = means double, # means triple, parentheses mark branches, and matching digits mark ring closures (e.g. c1ccccc1 for benzene). The formula you enter must match the atoms implied by your SMILES (including implicit hydrogens), or the submission will be rejected — this keeps the library self-consistent.
